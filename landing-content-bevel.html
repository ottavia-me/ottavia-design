Narrative Strategy

Story Structure

Classic three-act: establish the problem (the world gamifies your body), introduce the turn (what if something just listened), deliver the proof (Ottavia in action). Each beat is one full-screen scroll panel.

Brand Stance

"Your body is not a competition" is the line that positions Ottavia against every fitness app that ranks, gamifies, and reduces health to leaderboards. It's a belief, not a tagline.

Emotional Arc

Frustration (I have all this data and it doesn't help) → Recognition (wait, someone gets it) → Trust (this thing actually knows me) → Action (I want in).

Voice Rules

Short sentences. No adverbs. No hype words. No exclamation marks. Let the specificity do the persuading. If a sentence could come from any health app, cut it.

01 Hook

The Opening

Narrative Intent

Stop the scroll. Make a claim that feels personal, not corporate. The visitor should feel like someone just said something they've been thinking but haven't heard anyone say out loud. No logo, no product name — just the belief.

Headline
Your body is not
a competition.
Supporting Line

It's a conversation. One that's been happening your whole life. You just haven't had the right listener.

Alternative Supporting Lines
A It's a system. One that runs 24 hours a day, whether you're paying attention or not. What if something else was?
B It's the most complex system you'll ever own. And no app has ever tried to actually understand it.
C It doesn't need a coach. It needs a listener. One that connects what your sleep, training, and food are actually saying.
Design Direction
Full viewport. No navigation visible. No logo. Just the statement, large, left-aligned. The supporting line fades in 0.5s after the headline. A subtle "scroll" indicator at the bottom. The nav wordmark and CTA fade in only after the user begins scrolling.
On-Screen Elements

02 Tension

The Scoreboard Problem

Narrative Intent

Name the enemy without naming a competitor. The enemy is the pattern — the gamification model that every fitness app uses. Steps, streaks, badges, rankings. The visitor should nod and think "yeah, that's exactly what bothers me." Build resentment toward the status quo.

Headline
Every app you've tried
turned your health into a scoreboard.
Body

Steps counted. Calories burned. Streaks maintained. Badges earned. Rankings climbed.

All of it designed to make you do more. None of it designed to help you understand more.

Alternative Body Copy
A You've been scored, ranked, and gamified. You've maintained streaks you didn't care about and lost streaks that made you feel like you failed. None of it made you healthier. It made you more compliant.
B More steps than yesterday. Longer streak than last month. Higher ranking than your friends. But do you actually understand your body any better than you did a year ago?
Voice Note
The rhythm of "Steps counted. Calories burned." is deliberate — short, staccato, building. It should feel like a list that's exhausting to read. That exhaustion is the point.
On-Screen Elements
heading Two-line headline, second line dimmed
body Two short paragraphs
03 Tension

The Data Paradox

Narrative Intent

Shift from emotional frustration to intellectual frustration. The problem isn't that you don't have data — it's that you have too much, spread across too many apps, and none of it connects. This is the setup for Ottavia's core value prop: cross-domain reasoning.

Headline
You have more data
about your body
than any generation in history.
Body

Your watch knows your heart rate variability. Your sleep stages. Your training load. Your food app knows your macros. Your scale knows your weight.

But none of them talk to each other. And none of them know you.

Alternative Closing Lines
A You have five apps and zero understanding.
B The data exists. The connections don't.
C Every app sees one piece. Nobody sees the whole picture.
Watch Out
Don't make this feel like a tech problem ("siloed data" / "fragmented ecosystem"). Make it feel personal. The frustration is: I'm wearing a $400 watch and I still don't know if I should run today.
On-Screen Elements
heading Three-line headline, last line dimmed
body Two paragraphs, second one is the punch
04 Turn

The Question

Narrative Intent

This is the pivot. The mood shifts. The background goes dark. We stop naming problems and start opening a door. This beat is a question, not an answer — it creates pull. The visitor leans in because they want to know what "paying attention" looks like.

Headline
What if something
just paid attention?
Body

Not to your steps. Not to your streaks. To the patterns between your sleep and your training and your nutrition that only appear when someone looks at all three together.

The things you can't see by staring at one dashboard at a time.

Alternative Headlines
A What if something actually listened?
B What if the answer was already in your data?
C What if something connected the dots?
Design Direction
Background transitions from cream to dark (ink). This is the threshold — everything before was the old world, everything after is Ottavia's world. The shift should feel like walking into a quiet room. No animation fireworks. Just a color change and a question.
On-Screen Elements
heading Two-line question, second line dimmed
body Two short paragraphs
bg Transition to dark
05 Reveal

Ottavia Enters

Narrative Intent

First time the product name appears. The word "Introducing" is small and understated — the product doesn't announce itself loudly. The headline IS the value proposition, stated as what the product does, not what it is. The green accent color appears for the first time on the key phrase.

Eyebrow
Introducing
Headline
Ottavia connects your sleep,
training and nutrition to find
patterns that only appear
when they're connected.
Body

Not a dashboard. Not a chatbot. A reasoning system that remembers everything you share, grows sharper over time, and tells you what it actually thinks.

Alternative Body Copy
A A personal intelligence system. It connects your wearable data across domains, reasons over your history, and gives you the kind of specific, honest guidance that only comes from actually knowing someone.
B One system that sees your sleep, your training, and your nutrition together. That remembers your last three months. That tells you when to push and when to stop — and means it.
Voice Note
"Not a dashboard. Not a chatbot." — the negation pattern is important here. The visitor has been burned by things that call themselves "AI health assistants." We need to preemptively reject those categories before they lump us in.

06 Proof

The Conversation

Narrative Intent

Show, don't tell. Two real conversation exchanges that demonstrate specificity, directness, and data-grounded reasoning. The first exchange shows Ottavia saying "no" (the hardest, most trust-building thing). The second shows Ottavia connecting dots across domains. No UI chrome — just the words. The product proves itself through language.

Section Label
This is what it sounds like
Exchange 1 — The "No"
Should I run today?
Ottavia No. Your HRV is 38 — lowest in two weeks. Yesterday's tempo run on four days of caloric deficit caught up. Rest today. The long run can move to Saturday when your body has had two recovery nights.
Exchange 2 — The Connection
How's my nutrition been?
Ottavia You've hit 130g protein three days straight — that's the target we set together. Your recovery scores are responding. Up 8% since you started hitting it consistently. Keep this up through the weekend.
Alternative Exchange Ideas
A "Why was yesterday's run so hard?" → Ottavia connects sleep deficit + high training load + low carb intake from the day before
B "What should I eat before my long run?" → Ottavia references the user's past meal timing patterns and what worked best for their specific body
C A proactive morning message: "Your sleep dipped to 5.8 hours. Combined with yesterday's threshold session, today is a recovery day. Here's what that means for your week."
Voice Note
These examples must be indistinguishable from real Ottavia output. Use real-sounding numbers (HRV 38, not HRV 40). Reference specific timeframes (two weeks, three days). Include the cross-domain reasoning (deficit + tempo run = rest). If an example could come from any generic AI, replace it.

07 Proof

The Counter-Narrative

Narrative Intent

Crystallize the difference. A 2x3 grid where every cell crosses out the old-world pattern and replaces it with the Ottavia way. This should feel like a manifesto compressed into a table. "It's you vs. you" reappears here as the section headline — connecting the hero statement to the product difference.

Headline
It's you vs. you.
And Ottavia is on your side.
Grid Content
Leaderboards and rankings
Patterns only visible in your data
Generic advice for everyone
Insights that only apply to you
Streaks and badges
Understanding that compounds
"Great job! Keep it up! 💪"
"Don't run today. Rest."
Disconnected dashboards
Sleep + training + nutrition, connected
One-size-fits-all
Remembers your last three months
Watch Out
The crossed-out items should never feel like we're attacking specific apps. They're patterns, not products. "Leaderboards" is fair game. "Strava's leaderboards" is not. The contrast should feel like a worldview difference, not a competitive takedown.

08 Proof

What It Does

Narrative Intent

Functional proof. After the emotional story, the visitor needs to know what they'll actually get. Three cards, three features, no fluff. The transition back to cream background signals we're moving from the emotional argument to the practical one. Keep the copy tight — each card is one capability, one sentence of context.

Section Label
What Ottavia Does
Headline
Morning reports. Post-workout analysis.
Meal feedback in seconds.
Connections you can't see alone.
Card 1 — Morning Reports

Morning Reports. After your sleep syncs, Ottavia tells you how you recovered and what today can look like. Not a score. A plan.

Card 2 — Post-Workout

Post-Workout Analysis. Connects your effort to your recent load, sleep and nutrition. Was that run harder than it should have been? Ottavia knows why.

Card 3 — Meal Feedback

Meal Feedback. Snap a photo or just tell her. Seconds later, you know how it fits your targets and what it means for your recovery tonight.

Optional 4th Card
A Proactive Cues. Ottavia doesn't wait for you to ask. When your data says something important, she tells you. A morning note. A post-training nudge. The kind of thing a coach who sees your data would say.
Design Direction
Background transitions back to cream. Three white cards in a row. Keep cards compact — heading + two sentences max. No icons, no illustrations. The words carry it.

09 Emotional Anchor

The Line

Narrative Intent

The single moment that should stick in someone's memory after they leave the page. This isn't a feature. It's a story compressed into three lines. It demonstrates everything the brand stands for: specificity (HRV 30), honesty (don't run), and the courage to say no. Centered. Quiet. No body copy. Just the line.

Full Text
Ottavia told a runner to rest
when his HRV dropped to 30.

He wanted to run.

She said don't.
Alternative Versions
A "Ottavia told a cyclist to eat more when her deficit hit day five. She didn't want to. Ottavia showed her the recovery cost. She ate."
B "Ottavia noticed a pattern: every Monday, his HRV crashed. Three weeks of data. Same cause. Sunday long runs on Saturday night sleep. She told him. He moved the run to Saturday morning. Mondays recovered."
Voice Note
The power is in the structure: statement → conflict → resolution in three lines. "She said don't." is the shortest, most confident sentence on the entire page. It earns its weight from everything that came before it. Don't add to it. Don't explain it.
