The food log as a record

v1 answered how food gets in (composer-native, no modality step, log-then-context). This one is about where it lives: the day view a person opens at 4pm asking "how am I doing, and what should dinner be?" Three questions: (1) What shape is a day — ledger, grouped meals, or a lens on the chat? (2) Which numbers earn a place at the top? (3) How do editing, empty days, and past days work? Built on Redesign v1 tokens, with Elad's sample data (2,200 kcal · 115 g protein · 2,500 ml water).

Q1 · Day shapeChronological ledger vs grouped-by-meal vs a filtered view of the conversation itself.
Q2 · The numbersThe current screen shows every nutrient with equal weight. What actually earns a bar?
Q3 · States & editEmpty day, fixing a portion after the fact, and what yesterday looks like.

Question 1
What shape is a day?
The log is read at two moments: mid-day ("how am I doing → what's my next move?") and end-of-day / retrospectively ("what did I actually eat?"). The shape should serve the first moment without failing the second.
Lean: Direction B — Ottavia's one-line judgment on top, grouped meals below — is the spine. It answers "how am I doing" before showing a single number, which is the product's whole premise. Take Direction A's row anatomy (time · thumbnail · name · kcal) as the meal-row component inside the groups. Direction C shouldn't be a view, but its principle survives as a behavior: every meal row deep-links back to its chat moment — the log is an index of the conversation, not a second database.
Direction A — The ledger
Strictly chronological, dense, scannable. The day as a receipt: time, item, calories, running total pinned at the bottom. Fastest answer to "what did I eat?"

Question 2
Which numbers earn a place?
Cognitive-load call. Nutrition data is a long tail — the current screen renders ~15 nutrients with identical visual weight and fake targets. The alternatives: show only what has a real goal behind it, or go further and show only the judgment.
Lean: Direction B — a number gets a bar only if the user has a goal for it (kcal, protein, water today; fiber if a goal exists). Everything else lives behind one "All nutrients" row. Direction C's sentence is the right top layer but wrong as the only layer — athletes will want the bars without a tap. Direction A is the current screen, kept here honestly as the thing we're arguing against: equal weight for caffeine and protein is a hierarchy failure, and 82/100 caffeine reads as a goal nearly met.
Direction A — Everything (status quo)
Six stat tiles + the full nutrient list vs a placeholder target of 100. Complete, honest about the data — and unreadable as a hierarchy. What matters today is invisible.

Question 3
Empty days, wrong portions, and yesterday
The states that make a log trustworthy. An empty day shouldn't scold; a wrong portion must be fixable in seconds without re-logging; a past day should read as a record, not a to-do list.
Lean: the empty state seeds from frequent foods (the data already exists in FrequentFoods.tsx) — one tap re-logs a usual meal, which is the single highest-leverage friction cut for a daily habit. Portion fix is a sheet over the row (Direction B), with live macro recalb — never a navigation away. Past days keep the exact same layout with the day sentence rewritten in past tense; the ghost "dinner not logged" row disappears — a record has no to-dos.
State — Empty day
Nothing logged by 11am. No zeros-grid, no guilt. One suggestion cluster from the user's own history, and the composer stays the universal entry.

Product thinking
Hard problems & what to test first
1 · One database, two surfaces

If the chat logs it and the Nutrition screen shows it, they must never disagree. A meal edited in the log must be the same record the chat confirmation referenced.

→ The log is an index of chat-logged records, not a parallel store. Every row deep-links to its chat moment.
2 · Real targets or no bars

Bars against placeholder targets (today's target: 100) actively misinform — caffeine at 82/100 reads as a goal nearly met.

→ Rule: a bar renders only when a goal exists in preference.json. No goal, no bar — value shown as plain data.
3 · Auto-grouping will be wrong sometimes

"Breakfast/lunch/dinner" from timestamps breaks for shift workers, fasters, and a 23:40 bowl of cereal.

→ Group by time heuristics, but the group label is editable on the meal — and never used for judgment, only layout.
4 · The day boundary

A meal logged at 00:30 after a late dinner belongs to "tonight," not "tomorrow." Wearable days and calendar days already disagree.

→ Day cut at ~04:00 local (like sleep tracking), not midnight. Test against Elad's real logs.
5 · Retro-logging honesty

People log lunch at 9pm. If the ledger timestamps it 21:14, the record lies about the day.

→ On log, Ottavia infers "was this just now?" — one-tap adjust ("that was lunch") sets eaten-time separate from logged-time.
6 · Empathy on the over days

A 2,900-kcal Saturday will happen. A red bar and a minus number is shaming; pretending it didn't happen breaks trust.

→ Past-tense sentence states the fact + the recovery frame ("one high day; the week is still on trend"). Never red, never streak-breaking.
